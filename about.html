Our Company

Nearly four decades of making work safer

What began in 1988 as a trading company in the Korea World Trade Center, Seoul, is today a vertically integrated safety and engineering group serving customers across four continents.

Company History

Built step by step, factory by factory

Salus grew the way we advise our customers to grow: by mastering one process at a time. Trading gave us market knowledge. Knitting gave us capacity. Coating gave us quality leadership. Engineering made us self-sufficient — and turned our know-how into a product of its own.

Milestones

Our journey

1988

Hanyang Enterprise founded

Established as a trading company in the Korea World Trade Center, Seoul, dealing in textiles, plastics machinery and parts.

2001

Knitting factory established

A dedicated knitting plant opens with 100 SHIMA SEIKI machines, exporting gloves to Europe, the USA, Canada, Japan and the U.A.E.

2005

Coating technology breakthrough

Successful in-house development of advanced glove-coating technology — the foundation of today's engineering division.

2014

SALUS SAFETY INC is born

New wing-type coating machines minimise defects; the company is renamed SALUS SAFETY INC and capacity rises to 1.2 million pairs per month.

2020

Respiratory protection line

Face mask machinery is engineered in-house and KF94 / KF80 mask production begins, with FDA certification application filed.

Today

Two divisions, one mission

Hand Protection and Engineering Solutions operate side by side across four specialised factories — protecting people and transforming industry.

Sustainability

Cleaner chemistry, longer leaching, less waste

Our wing-type coating lines use 70–80 metre leaching tanks that remove residual DMF far more effectively than conventional plants — better for workers, wearers and the environment. Silicone-free PU chemistry, water-based pigments for NBR and latex, and defect-minimising process design reduce waste at every stage.

  • Extended leaching for superior DMF removal
  • Water-based colour pigments
  • Defect-rate reduction as an environmental policy
